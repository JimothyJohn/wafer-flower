OP 010

CAD — one segment, 6 features

  1. Variables: #R, #theta, #Ri, #bw, #tmin, #bond from the calculator above once it looks right. Sector angle is 360/#N (40° at N=9).
  2. Own wafer plane: through the radial line at (#R, 0), angled #theta from Top. Offset a copy by −(t_wafer/2 + #bond) → the land plane.
  3. Neighbor plane: the own wafer plane revolved −360/#N about center (model it directly: radial line at that angle, same construction). Offset by −(t_wafer/2 + 3) → the clearance plane.
  4. Blank: sector 360/#N × Ri→Ri+bw, extruded tall (60). Flat bottom at z = −(y_max·tanθ + t_wafer/2 + #bond + #tmin) where y_max = (Ri+bw)·sin(180/#N) — gives the taper with your minimum thickness at the trailing edge. The t_wafer/2 term is not optional: the land plane is offset by t/2 + bond, so dropping it leaves the base slab protruding 0.39 mm above the land at the trailing outer corner, pressing into the bondline.
  5. Two cuts: remove everything above the land plane, then everything above the clearance plane. The surviving top is bondable only where the land plane governs.
  6. Dovetails on the radial end faces (12/16 × 8, female +0.25). See OP 015 — if you plan to hang from one point, use two dovetails per face.
  7. Jig keyhole (Rev B.3): Ø5 radial through-hole in from the outer arc face on the segment centreline (a = 0), axis at z1 + 2.6 — 12.6 mm above the flat bottom. Through, not blind: the OP 012 cure jig passes a #10-24 rod to a fence on the inboard side, and the raised axis lets a wing nut swing over the bench. Exits the inner face 0.1 mm above the gear flange (teeth untouched), keeps a 2.4 mm web under the adhesive pocket floor, and never touches the land. Ream the printed hole Ø5 (13/64" works). Illustrated step-by-step for the jig itself: cure-jig.html.
  8. Gear flange: a planar annular sector, 6 mm thick, sitting on the flat bottom and extending inward from Ri to the gear root circle. Cut an internal gear into its inner edge. The flat bottom is the only planar face this part has, which is exactly why the flange goes there — see the note below.
This is now a solid model
scripts/segment_stl.py (needs pip install manifold3d) builds the segment by CSG and writes watertight STLs — segment.stl, segment_pair.stl (gate T1), halo_frame.stl, halo_assembly.stl. Every parameter above is a command-line flag: --theta 5 --Ri 270. Measured off the generated solid: T1 clearance exactly 3.000 mm, dovetail/socket interference 0 mm³, segment 217.0 cm³ → 124 g at 45 % infill, frame 1.12 kg + 1.15 kg of silicon = 2.27 kg.

Two things the solid corrected. The part is 37.2 mm tall, not the 44.5 mm the taper formula predicts — that formula is the un-cut wedge and ignores the clearance cut, which removes the top corner. And the clearance disc must be cut 2 mm wider than the wafer: at exactly Ø300 the cut wall lands tangent to the neighbour's rim, leaving zero lateral clearance against T6's ±0.5 mm centring and the wafer's own ±0.2 mm diameter tolerance.
Why the gear cannot go on the rim
Both the inner and outer edges of the segment follow the tilted land, so they swing ±15 mm out of the ring plane across one sector — 30 mm peak to peak. A fixed pinion cannot mesh with teeth that wander 30 mm axially. The flat bottom is the segment's only planar surface, so the ring gear has to live on a flange there. Tooth count must divide by N or the pitch breaks across every joint: at module 2 with N=9 that means 28 teeth per segment = 252 total (pitch Ø504, tips at r=250), which needs Ri ≈ 255. At Ri=248 the only fit is 243T and the tips clear the hide window by just 1 mm — inside print tolerance. The calculator flags this live.
Design change vs Rev A
The neighbor clearance cut (step 3/5) is mandatory. Without it, each segment's land near its leading joint rises ~2·ρ·sin(180/N)·tanθ above the adjacent wafer's plane and collides with it (34 mm of interference at θ=10°, N=9, ρ=280). The cut costs a ~6° corner of land; ~34° of bondable arc remains.
OP 015

Statics — what actually carries the load

Everything below is computed live in the calculator's right column. This section says what each number means and which ones you're allowed to ignore. Short version: gravity is a rounding error, thermal mismatch is 40× gravity, and the printed dovetail is the tightest margin in the build.

1 · Load decomposition at one station

The ring hangs in a vertical plane, so gravity lies in the ring plane. Each wafer's plane is tilted θ about its radial axis, so its unit normal is n = (sinθ·sinα, −sinθ·cosα, cosθ) for a station at ring angle α. Resolving weight W onto that plane:

normal (peel/compression) W_n = W·sinθ·|cos α| → max at 12 and 6 o'clock in-plane (shear) W_s = W·√(1 − sin²θ·cos²α) → 0.985·W … W

At θ=10° the bond sees essentially pure shear at 98.5–100 % of W, with at most 17 % of W trying to pull it off. Both are small: a Ø300 wafer is 1.25 N spread over ~96 cm² of land, i.e. 0.13 kPa shear against a capability near 500 kPa. Gravity is ~4000× under-stressed. Stop worrying about it.

2 · The moment nobody draws: land centroid vs wafer CG

The wafer's centroid sits at pitch radius R. The bond land sits between Ri and Ri+bw, and the clearance cut chews an asymmetric corner off its leading end — so the land centroid is both inboard of the wafer CG and rotated trailing of it. At the current Rev B numbers the land centroid lands at ρ=271 and the offset is 79 mm. The normal load component acting on that arm is a peel couple:

M_peel = W·sinθ·|cos α| · d d = |centroid_land − centroid_wafer|, measured in the wafer plane σ_peel = M_peel / S S = I/c of the land patch about the axis ⊥ to d

The good news is that S for the 96 cm² patch is ~64 000 mm³, so σ_peel lands at 0.27 kPa — still nothing. The bad news is that the offset is not free: it puts 197 mm of wafer outboard of any support (§3), and once the thing rotates, that peel term reverses sign every revolution (§6). Use the Center under wafer CG button to see the alternative — the hide window at θ=10° runs to 416 mm, so a band at ~322–377 is legal and pulls the offset from 79 mm to 14 mm. It is not free. The band moves to a larger radius, so the segment gets bigger and heavier: mass goes 2.35 → 2.90 kg (+23 %), the land grows 96 → 120 cm² which raises thermal shear 6.0 → 7.8 kPa (§4: stress tracks land size), and the extra mass drags the dovetail margin 2.0× → 1.6× (§5). Centering buys you droop and kills the reversing peel term (§6) — take it if you're fixing the dovetail anyway, which you should be.

3 · The wafer as a cantilever

Outboard of the land's outer edge, the wafer is an unsupported plate of thickness t carrying its own weight. Treated as a unit-width cantilever with only the plane-normal gravity component:

Stress margin is 9–50×; silicon is not going to break under its own weight. Mounted droop of 0.11 mm against a 3 mm gap is not a live constraint at the Rev B design point — but it scales as L⁴/t², so it turns into one fast if you push the band inboard or widen it (at Ri=248, bw=90 the overhang reaches 0.50 mm mounted and 2.9 mm flat, which is the whole clearance budget on the bench). Watch the readout rather than the rule of thumb.

Handling, not statics
Self-weight is 0.60 MPa. A point load at the free edge is a different story: spreading the moment over an effective width of ~0.5–1.0 L gives σ ≈ 6PL/(b_eff·t²) ≈ 4–13 MPa per newton, so a 2–3 N push (a 250 g finger) at the free edge reaches the 30 MPa allowable. This is why OP 020 says press over the land centroid only — the analysis says the wafer survives gravity, not you.

4 · Thermal — the governing load, quantified

Δα ≈ 60 − 2.6 ≈ 57 ppm/K (PETG vs Si) slip at the land's far corner s = Δα·ΔT·L_max L_max = centroid→farthest bond point shear strain γ = s / bondline shear stress τ_th = G_bond · γ

At ΔT=20 K and L_max = 96 mm: s = 110 µm, γ = 10 % on a 1.1 mm foam bondline, τ = 6.0 kPa with G=0.06 MPa. That is ~47× the gravity shear — thermal is the design load, gravity is noise. Drag the G slider to see the fork:

Note what the formula says about geometry: τ_th scales with L_max, not with area. A bigger bond patch is a worse bond patch. Two 25 mm arcs near the land centroid beat full coverage — that's not a materials-saving heuristic, it's the actual physics. It also means wafer size doesn't drive the thermal load; land size does.

5 · The ring as a structure — and the real weak link

Nine stations at 128 g of silicon plus 133 g of PETG each (gear flange included — slicer-measured at 45 % infill, not the 127 g the uniform-density estimate gave) is 2.35 kg / 23.0 N. Hung from a single point at 12 o'clock, treat each half-ring as a curved cantilever (conservative — a closed ring is stiffer and runs ~0.3× this):

M_max ≈ W_total·R/π ≈ 23.0 N × 0.35 m / π ≈ 2.6 N·m at the anchor joints

Through the full band section (55 mm deep × 6 mm) that's 0.8 MPa — irrelevant. But the joint doesn't have the full band section. A single centered dovetail connects only 16 mm of the band's 55 mm radial depth, so the section modulus collapses:

S_joint = t·w²/6 = 6 × 16²/6 = 256 mm³ σ = 2560 N·mm / 256 = 10.0 MPa allowable, printed PETG loaded across layers ≈ 20 MPa → margin 2.0×
Tightest margin in the build
2.0× on a printed dovetail root — a stress concentration, in the print's weak direction, under sustained load with PETG creep. Everything else in this analysis has 47–4000× margin; this has 2. Three fixes, cheapest first: (a) hang from two points at ±50° from top (drops M ~4×), (b) two dovetails per face, one near each band edge (restores most of the 55 mm depth, ~10× S), (c) rest the ring on a ledge instead of hanging it (compression arch, M → near zero). Option (b) is also what the motor drive wants — see below.

Out-of-plane, the frame's centroid sits well behind the wafer plane, so the assembly CG is 6.5 mm off the wall plane → a 0.14 N·m tip-off moment. Trivial for a wall hanger; it's the number the motor bearing will care about.

7 · Settling θ = 5 vs 10

This was the open question, and the statics answer it cleanly: θ is an aesthetic choice, and every structural metric prefers the smaller one. Ø300, N=9, band centered under the wafer CG:

θEnvelope depthNeighbor z-gapDroop (hung)Assembly massDovetail margin
5°26.9 mm14.3 mm0.01 mm2.31 kg2.0×
7.5°39.9 mm21.5 mm0.01 mm2.60 kg1.8×
10°52.9 mm28.9 mm0.02 mm2.90 kg1.6×
15°78.4 mm44.2 mm0.02 mm3.48 kg1.3×

The z-gap argument for a larger θ evaporates: even θ=5 gives 14.3 mm of neighbor gap against a 3 mm requirement, and droop is ~0.01 mm either way. What θ actually buys is depth — how far the piece stands off the wall and how much the swirl reads as three-dimensional — at a cost of ~4 % assembly mass and ~5 % joint margin per degree (slicer-calibrated masses — the solid shells dilute the old uniform-density trade). Land arc is 36° of 40° at every θ in this range, so bond area isn't in the trade either.

Recommendation from the numbers: keep Ø300 — mass is a non-issue (2–3 kg) and it's the look you want. Pick θ purely on how deep you want the piece to sit; θ=10 is a fine choice and costs ~26 % more mass than θ=5, but nothing structural forces it. Move the band outboard toward the wafer CG only in the same revision that fixes the joint. And fix the dovetail first — it is the only number in this document under 40×, and every other improvement makes it worse.

Why T3 is the gate
Gravity shear on the bond is 0.13 kPa against a ~500 kPa capability — a ~4000× margin. The load case that matters is the 10–30 °C swing driving 110 µm of differential CTE slip at the land's far corner, ~47× the gravity stress and rising linearly with land size. A compliant bond absorbs it as ~10 % shear strain; a rigid bond puts it into the silicon. T3 with real thermal cycles is the only test that proves your specific adhesive on your specific print surface. Full-surface coverage is actively harmful: area was never the limit, and longer bonds see more end slip.

Bond — bench, one station at a time

  1. IPA-wipe land and wafer contact zone; let flash.
  2. Tape path: two 20–25 mm strips arced on the land, ≥5 mm inside its boundary, as close to the land centroid as the geometry allows (§4: stress scales with distance from centroid, not with area). Roller-press. Liners off only with wafer in hand. Liquid path (SMP): serpentine bead, 5 mm inside the land edge; Ø4 × #bond printed nubs (3×) set the bondline.
  3. Rest wafer edge on the jig's downhill fence first, lower like closing a book. Confirm both fences touch before full contact — tape gives one attempt.
  4. Press over the land centroid through a foam pad (tape: 15 s firm; SMP: clamp to nub contact + ¼ turn, 24 h in jig). Never press the overhang — 20 N at the free edge is ~35 MPa in the silicon, at the fracture allowable.
  5. Repeat ×N, then join the ring.

Motion — the halo as its own ring gear

The segment's flat bottom carries a planar flange, and an internal ring gear is cut into its inner edge at pitch Ø504. Tips land at r=250, still inside the hide window, so the whole drive is invisible from the front. A 28-tooth pinion sits inside the ring with its axis at r=224 — well within the Ø500 central hole — so the motor, the rollers and the wall anchor all live behind the halo on one hub plate. That is the version where the motor really can be the anchor.

Torque is not the problem — the load path is. A pinion mesh cannot be the hanging anchor: 23.0 N through a tooth flank with backlash means the ring drops, rocks, and clicks once per tooth. Split the two jobs:

  1. Three V-groove bearings ride the frame's rim as a rail — two below at ±120° carrying the weight in compression (arch action, which also collapses the §5 joint moment toward zero), one above as a keeper against tip-off.
  2. The pinion only supplies torque, spring-loaded into mesh so backlash never unloads. All four contact points live behind the wafer overhang.
  3. The bearing plate is the wall anchor. It sees 23.0 N shear plus the 0.14 N·m out-of-plane tip-off moment computed above — a two-screw plate into a stud, not a slewing ring.
Rotation changes the analysis
Everything in OP 015 is a static case at a fixed ring angle α. Spin the halo and α sweeps continuously, so the peel term W·sinθ·cos α·d fully reverses every revolution — each bond goes from compression to tension and back. At 0.3 rpm that is ~158 000 cycles/year. The amplitude is tiny (±0.27 kPa), and silicon does not fatigue, but a viscoelastic adhesive under fully-reversed load ratchets and creeps in a way a static hang never reveals. If motion is on the table, T3 becomes a cyclic test: same coupon, load reversed ~10⁴ times, then check the witness line. And it makes the case for centering the band under the wafer CG much stronger — kill the arm d and the reversing term goes with it.

Also: a rotating ring makes the single-point-hang joint case obsolete (rollers put the ring in compression), which retires the 2.2× dovetail margin — the tightest number in the build. Motion is arguably a structural simplification, not just a feature.

Square nuts are the right call — and here is the trap that fits one
Your preference is well founded, and the reason is bridging. A captured nut pocket is entered from a side face and printed over, so the slicer has to bridge its ceiling. A square pocket bridges a constant-width flat; a hex pocket's ceiling narrows across the span and its two upper flats become unsupported overhangs, which is why hex traps come out fuzzy and end up needing a reamer. Square also gives more anti-rotation purchase per millimetre of pocket depth.

Trap geometry, driven off the nut you actually buy (s = across flats, m = thickness):
pocket width s + 0.25 (M4 DIN 562 → 7.25 mm) pocket depth m + 0.20 (M4 DIN 562 → 2.40 mm, DIN 557 → 3.40 mm) entry slot s + 0.25 wide, run it to a side face so the nut drops in ceiling ≥ 3 solid layers over the pocket, no infill bore thread + 0.4 clearance, concentric with the pocket
Buy 2: the first one you press in at the wrong depth is a write-off, and nylon nuts strip long before steel does.
Scaling to other configurations
Filament is the only line that moves much. At N=9, θ=10°: Ø150 → 236 g of segments (1 spool), Ø200 → 438 g (1 spool), Ø300 → 1195 g (2 spools), all at 45 % infill, slicer-calibrated — small segments run shell-dominated, so they land above the old uniform-density figures. Wafer count follows N. Tape length is 50 mm per station regardless of wafer size, because pad size is set by the bond physics (§4), not by area.
Two pads, not one — and stagger them radially
Two 25 × 25 mm pads give 1250 mm² per station: 18.8 N of sustained shear capacity against 1.25 N applied, a 15× margin. Area was never the constraint. But place one pad near Ri and one near Ro rather than side by side at mid-band — the peel moment (§2) acts about the tangential axis, and radial separation is what gives the pad pair any section modulus against it. Two pads at the same radius are ~12× weaker in peel than the full land; staggered, ~6×. Both pass, but the stagger is free.
